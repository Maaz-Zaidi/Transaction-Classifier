# Phase 9 Results: Rules engine overhaul and token-aware query pipeline

**Date:** 2026-04-05

## Motivation

Phase 8 delivered 73.86% unique accuracy on the openai test set. With the knowledge base and retrieval pipeline now in place, the system had matured to the point where two pieces of early scaffolding needed to be retired.

The first was the merchant-identity rules in the rules engine. During earlier phases, before the knowledge base existed, I added about 60 rules that mapped specific brand names directly to categories. COSTCO to Food and Dining, AMAZON to Shopping and Retail, UBER to Transportation, and so on. These were useful as training wheels. They gave the pipeline reasonable coverage on common merchants while I focused on building out the ML model, the preprocessing pipeline, and eventually the external knowledge base. They were never intended to be permanent. The plan was always to let the KB and ML handle merchant identity once those components were mature enough.

By Phase 8, the KB was handling merchant identity well (97.0% accuracy on its hits) and the metadata enrichment path was operational. The merchant-identity rules had served their purpose. But they were also now causing problems. In Phase 8, rules accounted for 120 of the 505 test predictions at 84.17% accuracy, meaning 19 predictions were wrong. These errors came from the inherent limitation of mapping a brand name to a single category without context. A transaction like COSTCO GAS was being classified as Food and Dining because COSTCO matched a rule, even though the transaction was clearly a gas station purchase. UBER EATS was classified as Transportation because UBER matched a rule, even though the actual transaction was food delivery. The rules had no way to handle these distinctions because they operated on brand identity alone.

Now that the KB and ML pipeline can handle merchant resolution with full context awareness, these scaffolding rules were no longer helping. They were intercepting transactions that should have been routed to the KB for proper metadata enrichment and then classified by the ML model.

The second issue was that the query pipeline treated all tokens equally. When a transaction like COSTCO GAS W1263 reached the knowledge base, the system would build an FTS query of `"COSTCO" OR "GAS" OR "W1263"` and a dense search over the full string. There was no awareness that COSTCO is a brand name (useful for KB lookup), GAS is a category-descriptive word (useful for ML enrichment), and W1263 is a branch identifier (noise). The flat token treatment was polluting both lexical and dense retrieval.

Phase 8 had already identified this issue in its weaknesses section. The lexical retrieval was too permissive because it included noise tokens. The dense retrieval was too influenced by location terms. The fusion layer was ranking wrong candidates because noise and location tokens were contributing signal.

## What I changed

### Rules engine overhaul

I rewrote `src/transaction_classifier/rules/rules.yaml` from scratch. I removed all merchant-identity rules, about 60 of them. What remains are approximately 25 rules that fall into three categories.

The first group is structural transaction patterns. These are patterns that describe the type of transaction itself, not the identity of the merchant. E-TRANSFER-OUT and E-TRANSFER-IN are synthetic tokens generated by preprocessing and unambiguously indicate a financial transfer. TRANSFER, MORTGAGE, LOAN PAYMENT, CREDIT CARD PAYMENT, NSF, OVERDRAFT, SERVICE CHARGE, and MONTHLY FEE are all financial service patterns that describe what the transaction is rather than who the merchant is. PAYROLL, SALARY, DEPOSIT, REFUND, and CASH BACK are income patterns. CRA, CANADA REVENUE, SERVICE CANADA, SERVICE ONTARIO, and GOV are government patterns.

The second group is generic service-type descriptors. These are words that, by themselves, denote a category regardless of the merchant. PARKING means Transportation. PHARMACY, DENTAL, HOSPITAL, CLINIC, and OPTOM mean Healthcare. GYM and FITNESS mean Entertainment and Recreation.

All merchant-identity rules were retired. TIM HORTONS, MCDONALD'S, STARBUCKS, SUBWAY, COSTCO, WALMART, LOBLAWS, AMAZON, NETFLIX, SPOTIFY, UBER, SHELL, ESSO, PETRO-CANADA, ROGERS, BELL, TELUS, CANADIAN TIRE, IKEA, HOME DEPOT, SHOPPERS DRUG MART, REXALL, OC TRANSPO, CINEPLEX, and about 35 more. These were the original scaffolding rules from earlier phases.

Now that the KB and ML pipeline are operational, these transactions should be handled by their proper components. A word like COSTCO tells you nothing about the category without external knowledge. COSTCO could be a grocery trip, a gas fill-up, a tire purchase, or a food court meal. The correct behavior is to look up COSTCO in the knowledge base, get its metadata, and let the ML model classify based on the full context.

### Refund prefix handling

I also fixed a preprocessing issue with refund transactions. Previously, a transaction like "REFUND - ZARA BAYSHORE OTTAWA ON" would pass through to the rules engine with REFUND still in the text, and the REFUND rule would classify it as Income. This is wrong. A refund from Zara is a shopping transaction.

The fix strips the refund prefix so the merchant name routes to the KB and ML pipeline. "REFUND - ZARA BAYSHORE OTTAWA ON" now becomes "ZARA BAYSHORE" after preprocessing. Standalone "REFUND" with no merchant name still maps to Income through the rules, which is correct.

This required two changes. First, the regex had to be placed after the bank prefix stripping step in the preprocessing pipeline. The raw text "Interac purchase refund - 5622 ZARA BAYSHORE" first gets "Interac purchase" stripped by the existing prefix patterns, leaving "REFUND - 5622 ZARA BAYSHORE", and then the refund regex strips the "REFUND - 5622" prefix. Second, the regex had to handle digits after the dash. Real bank data includes patterns like "refund-7352 UNDER ARMOUR OT" where a terminal ID immediately follows the dash.

The implementation is in `src/transaction_classifier/data/preprocess.py`, lines 136 to 142.

### Token analyzer

I created a new module at `src/transaction_classifier/knowledge/token_analyzer.py` that decomposes a cleaned transaction string into typed tokens.

Each token is classified as one of four roles:

- brand: part of the merchant name, useful for KB lookup
- descriptor: carries category-semantic signal, useful for ML enrichment
- location: a Canadian city or neighbourhood, noise for classification
- noise: branch IDs, short codes, pure numeric sequences

The classification uses a four-layer heuristic pipeline. First, noise is identified by regex patterns that match single characters, pure digits, and branch patterns like W1263 or S123 or P456. Second, locations are identified by set lookup against about 50 major Canadian cities and neighbourhoods. Most of these should already be stripped by preprocessing, but this catches any that leak through. Third, descriptors are identified by dictionary lookup against about 40 known category-descriptor words. GAS maps to "gas station, fuel". WHOLESALE maps to "wholesale warehouse shopping". DOWNLOADS maps to "digital content downloads". TUITION maps to "education tuition fees". GROCERY, RESTAURANT, PHARMACY, COFFEE, BAKERY, PIZZA, GRILL, SHAWARMA, and others are all mapped. There is also a multi-token descriptor dictionary that catches phrases like WEB SERVICES, DRUG MART, GAS BAR, and FINE FOODS. Fourth, everything else defaults to brand.

There is also a multi-token brand detection step that runs before individual classification. It checks if 2-token or 3-token combinations match KB aliases. This ensures that a merchant like CANADIAN TIRE is treated as a single brand, not as two separate tokens.

The analyzer produces a DecomposedQuery dataclass that contains the original text, the token roles, and two derived fields. brand_query is the reconstruction of only the brand tokens, intended for KB lookup. descriptor_context is a human-readable summary of the descriptor hints, intended for ML enrichment.

### Knowledge base integration

I added a `search_with_tokens()` method to `src/transaction_classifier/knowledge/merchant_kb.py` that wraps the existing search pipeline with token-aware improvements.

The method first calls the token analyzer to decompose the query. Then it tries exact alias lookup on the full cleaned text. If that fails, it tries exact alias on the brand-only query. For example, "COSTCO GAS W1263" decomposes to brand_query "COSTCO", and exact alias lookup on "COSTCO" succeeds even though the full string does not match any alias.

For lexical retrieval, I added `_fts_query_from_decomposed()` which builds the FTS query from brand and descriptor tokens only, excluding noise and location. This means a query like "COSTCO GAS W1263 KANATA" generates `"COSTCO" OR "GAS"` instead of `"COSTCO" OR "GAS" OR "W1263" OR "KANATA"`.

For dense retrieval, the method uses the brand_query instead of the full text. This gives the dense embedder a cleaner signal.

I also modified `build_enriched_transaction_text()` to accept a descriptor_context parameter. When present, it appends "descriptor context: gas station, fuel" to the enriched text that goes to the ML model. This means the ML receives both the brand metadata from the KB and the transaction-specific descriptor signal from token analysis.

### Ensemble integration

I integrated the token-aware pipeline into `src/transaction_classifier/models/ensemble.py`. The ensemble now creates a TokenAnalyzer in its constructor (gated by a `token_analysis_enabled` config setting) and calls `search_with_tokens()` instead of `search()` in the KB lookup section.

There is one new behavior in the ensemble. When the KB does not find a match but the token analyzer identifies descriptor tokens, the descriptor context is still injected into the ML input. For example, if "SOME UNKNOWN GAS STATION" has no KB match, the ML still receives "SOME UNKNOWN GAS STATION. descriptor context: gas station, fuel" instead of just the raw text. This gives the ML a stronger signal even when the merchant is not in the external database.

### Test changes

I rewrote `tests/test_rules.py` to remove all merchant-identity test cases and replace them with structural rule tests. I updated `tests/test_ensemble.py` to use structural rules (PARKING LOT, NSF FEE, DENTAL CLINIC) instead of merchant names (TIM HORTONS, NETFLIX, STARBUCKS). I updated `tests/test_api.py` similarly. I added refund test cases to `tests/test_preprocess.py`.

I created a new test file `tests/test_token_analyzer.py` with parametrized tests for token decomposition, location detection, brand query construction, descriptor context generation, multi-token descriptors, noise patterns, and edge cases.

I added tests to `tests/test_merchant_kb.py` for `search_with_tokens()`, including exact match with tokens, decomposed query verification, behavior without an analyzer, and `build_enriched_transaction_text()` with descriptor context.

The full test suite passes with 153 tests.

## Evaluation results

I also updated `scripts/evaluate_phase6.py` to load the knowledge base during evaluation. Previously, the evaluation script was not loading the KB at all, which meant the evaluation numbers in earlier phases were measuring rules plus raw ML only. This was inconsistent with how the API actually serves predictions.

I ran the evaluation on the same 505-row openai test set.

### Phase 8 baseline (for comparison)

The Phase 8 result was 73.86% unique accuracy across 505 test samples.

- Rules: 120 predictions at 84.17%
- Knowledge base: 100 predictions at 97.0%
- Fine-tune with metadata: 200 predictions at 67.5%
- Fine-tune only: 76 predictions at 40.8%

The Phase 9 result is 71.3% unique accuracy across 505 test samples.

Source breakdown:

- Rules: 13 predictions at 92.3%
- Knowledge base: 161 predictions at 91.3%
- Fine-tune with metadata: 240 predictions at 65.8%

Average confidence: 0.566. Inference time: 147.4 seconds.

By category:

### What the numbers mean

The headline number dropped from 73.86% to 71.3%, a decrease of 2.56 percentage points. This is the expected cost of retiring the scaffolding rules. Those 107 transactions that were previously handled by merchant-identity rules at 84.17% accuracy now route through the KB and ML pipeline, which is their intended long-term path.

Of those 107 transactions, roughly 61 found KB matches (KB total went from 100 to 161). The remaining 46 went to the ML model. The KB hits are at 91.3% accuracy, which is reliable but slightly lower than the Phase 8 figure of 97.0%. This small drop is expected because the new KB hits include harder cases that were previously caught by the rules.

The rules engine itself went from 120 predictions at 84.17% to 13 predictions at 92.3%. Only 1 of the 13 structural rule predictions was wrong (a false positive on "AZGA SERVICE CANADA INC" which matched the SERVICE CANADA government rule but was actually a financial services transaction). This is the behavior the rules engine was always meant to settle into. It is now precise and limited to its proper scope of structural and transactional patterns.

The fine-tune with metadata path increased from 200 to 240 predictions, with accuracy dropping from 67.5% to 65.8%. The increase in volume means more transactions are being routed through the enriched ML path, which is correct. The slight accuracy drop is likely because some of the newly routed transactions are harder cases.

The fine-tune only path (no KB match, no metadata) stayed roughly constant at 82 predictions and 41.5% accuracy. These are the transactions where neither the KB nor the rules can help, and the ML model has to classify from the raw text alone.

## Error analysis

I ran an error analysis on the 149 wrong predictions.

The fine-tune with metadata path produced 83 errors. These are cases where the KB found a match and enriched the text, but the ML model still classified incorrectly. Examples include AMAZON DOWNLOADS being classified as Shopping and Retail instead of Entertainment and Recreation, BEST BUY being classified as Utilities and Services instead of Shopping and Retail, and WMT SUPRCTR being classified as Utilities and Services instead of Shopping and Retail. Many of these have low confidence scores in the 0.2 to 0.4 range, indicating the model is uncertain.

The fine-tune only path produced 48 errors. These are cases with no KB match. Many are difficult strings like empty cleaned text, RIOT gaming transactions, PRESTO transit reloads, and obscure merchant abbreviations.

The knowledge base path produced 13 errors. Some of these are category mapping issues. COSTCO WHOLESALE is mapped to Shopping and Retail in the KB, but the ground truth labels these as Food and Dining (presumably grocery purchases). ONROUTE is mapped to Transportation, but it is actually a highway rest stop food court. POPEYES is mapped to Entertainment and Recreation, but it is a fast food restaurant. These are issues in the Foursquare category mapping, not in the retrieval itself.

The rules engine produced 5 errors, down from 19 in Phase 8. The drop from 19 to 5 reflects the removal of the scaffolding rules, which were the primary source of rules errors. Of the remaining 5, the refund fix eliminated 3 that were previously misclassified as Income. The remaining 2 are edge cases in the structural rules. One is "AZGA SERVICE CANADA INC" matching the SERVICE CANADA government rule, and the other is a similar false positive on a government pattern.

## Assessment

The overall accuracy decreased by 2.56 points, which represents the gap between the scaffolding and the real pipeline. The ML model is now doing the actual classification work. The knowledge base is the primary resolution mechanism, performing well at 91.3% accuracy on 161 matches. The metadata enrichment path is the largest single source of predictions at 240 transactions. This is the pipeline architecture I was building toward from the start. The rules were always a stopgap while the KB and ML components were being developed.

The bottleneck is clear. There are two areas where accuracy needs to improve.

First, the 82 transactions with no KB match rely entirely on the raw ML model, which gets 41.5%. These are either merchants not in the external database or transactions where the cleaned text is too short or ambiguous for retrieval to succeed.

Second, the 240 transactions going through metadata enrichment get 65.8%. The token-aware pipeline is injecting descriptor context into these, but the ML model is still struggling with many of them. The descriptor context helps in principle (a transaction like "COSTCO GAS" now gets "descriptor context: gas station, fuel" appended), but the fine-tuned MiniLM model has limited ability to use that context if it was not trained on similar enriched inputs.

The token analysis pipeline itself is working correctly. I verified that COSTCO GAS W1263 decomposes to brand COSTCO, descriptor GAS, noise W1263. AMAZON WEB SERVICES decomposes to brand AMAZON with multi-token descriptor WEB SERVICES. ZARA BAYSHORE decomposes to brand ZARA, location BAYSHORE. The FTS queries exclude noise and location tokens. The brand-only queries improve exact alias matching.

## Remaining work

The main remaining opportunities are:

1. Retrain the fine-tuned model on enriched inputs. The current model was trained on raw merchant names. If it were trained on text in the format "MERCHANT NAME. merchant identity: Full Name. external metadata: place types. descriptor context: descriptors", it would learn to use the enrichment signals directly.

2. Improve KB category mappings. Several high-confidence KB hits return wrong categories because the Foursquare category mapping is too coarse. COSTCO maps to Shopping but some COSTCO transactions are groceries. ONROUTE maps to Transportation but it is a food court.

3. Expand alias coverage. The 82 transactions with no KB match include merchants that likely exist in the Foursquare data under different names. Better alias generation during the KB build step would increase the hit rate.

4. Tune confidence thresholds. The current thresholds for direct KB resolution versus metadata enrichment versus raw ML were set before the rules overhaul. They may need adjustment now that the routing has changed.

## Conclusion

Phase 9 retired the merchant-identity scaffolding rules and introduced token-aware query construction. The headline accuracy dropped by 2.56 points, which is the expected short-term cost of removing the training wheels. The pipeline is now operating in its intended final architecture. The rules engine is limited to structural patterns. The knowledge base handles merchant resolution. The ML model handles classification. The token analyzer separates brand, descriptor, location, and noise tokens so that each component of the pipeline receives cleaner input.

The 2.56 point gap is a clear measure of the remaining work. The KB and ML need to fully absorb the responsibilities that the scaffolding rules were temporarily covering. Closing that gap through improved retrieval, better category mappings, and model retraining on enriched inputs is the next phase of work.
